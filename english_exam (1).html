✏️ Grammar
Rules and corrections from your lessons.
⏳
Used to / Be used to / Get used to
3 forms
▶
used to + infinitive
Past habit — no longer true today.
"I used to smoke but I quit five years ago." · "She used to walk to school every day."
be used to + -ing
Accustomed to something NOW — it doesn't feel strange anymore.
"She is used to working night shifts — it doesn't bother her." · "They are used to the cold weather."
get used to + -ing
The process of becoming accustomed — still happening.
"It's hard at first, but you'll get used to waking up early." · "He is getting used to the new schedule."
⚠ Common error
He was used to listen to loud music. → He was used to listening to loud music.
🔀
Passive Voice
4 tenses
▶
Present Simple Passive
is / are + past participle
"The report is reviewed every year." · "English is spoken all over the world."
Past Simple Passive
was / were + past participle
"The letter was sent yesterday morning." · "The building was built in 1905."
Present Continuous Passive
is / are being + past participle
"A new hospital is being built in the city centre."
Present Perfect Passive
has / have been + past participle
"The results have already been announced by the committee."
📅
Past Simple / Past Perfect / Past Continuous
3 tenses
▶
Past Simple
Completed action at a specific time in the past.
"She left at 8am." · "He called me yesterday."
Past Continuous
Action in progress at a specific moment in the past. Often interrupted by another action.
"He was reading a book when the phone rang." · "I was making dinner when I remembered the meeting."
Past Perfect
Action completed BEFORE another past action. The "earlier" past.
"When I arrived, she had already left." · "By the time we got there, the film had started."

Common Errors — Corrected
10 errors
▶
✗ I born
✓ I was born — always use passive for birth
✗ He loves see
✓ He loves seeing — after love/like/enjoy use -ing
✗ in the twenties
✓ in her twenties — always use possessive pronoun
✗ takes me arriving
✓ takes me to arrive — it takes + time + to + infinitive
✗ very sympathy
✓ very sympathetic — sympathy is a noun, not an adjective
✗ His death is come
✓ His death came — past simple, not present
✗ bill not include
✓ The bill does not include — needs auxiliary verb
✗ If they are agree
✓ If they agree — agree is a verb, not an adjective
✗ smoking in the outside
✓ smoking in the outdoor area — outside is an adverb; outdoor is the adjective
✗ told off to his son
✓ told his son off — tell off takes a direct object, no preposition

Stress & Tricky Sounds
7 words
▶
COT·tage
/ˈkɒt.ɪdʒ/
Stress on the FIRST syllable. The -age ending sounds like /ɪdʒ/, not /age/.
ma·TURE
/məˈtjʊər/
Stress on the SECOND syllable. The first 'a' is a very short schwa /ə/.
FOR·eign
/ˈfɒr.ɪn/
Stress on the FIRST syllable. The 'g' is completely SILENT. Say "FOR-in".
ALT·zhei·mer
/ˈælts.haɪ.mər/
Starts with /ælts/ — the 'z' sounds like /ts/. German origin.
wind
/wɪnd/ or /waɪnd/
wind (moving air) = /wɪnd/ (short i) · wind (to twist) = /waɪnd/ (long i, rhymes with "find")
STAN·dard
/ˈstæn.dərd/
Stress on the FIRST syllable. Short 'a' — /stæn/, not /staan/.
CON·stant·ly
/ˈkɒn.stənt.li/
Stress on the FIRST syllable. Adverbs ending in -ly almost always stress the root syllable.
